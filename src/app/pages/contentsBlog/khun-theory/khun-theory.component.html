Thomas Kuhn Theory

Thomas Kuhn (1922-1996) was an America historian and philosopher of science. He wrote one of the most influential and controversial books of the 20th century called ``The structure of Scientific Revolution``. His interest was in how scientists approach their daily work and think about the question of how Science develops over time.

Khun introduced the term “paradigm” as a set of universally recognized principles, methodological processes and cultural concepts that shape the science in a certain era. Kuhn argues that paradigms are incommensurable, meaning that there is no language or set of objective criteria that can be used to directly compare different paradigms. Each paradigm shapes reality in a unique way, making direct comparison between theories difficult. Nowadays, however, this term are expanded and used in another areas such as information technology, business, psychology, linguistic, and so on.

For Khun, Science develops in two phases, one called normal and another extraordinary or revolutionary. “Normal science” is periods of conceptual continuity where there is cumulative progress within a settled paradigm or explanatory framework. The normal science is interrupted by periods of revolutionary science. The discovery of "anomalies" during revolutions in science leads to new paradigms. New paradigms then raise new questions of old data, moving beyond the mere puzzle-solving of the previous paradigm, change the rules of the game and taking scientific research to new paths.

His main contribution was to bring about a significant chance about the way that people viewed scientific development. The standard view of scientific progression before Khun is that science is a piecemeal cumulative process (linear progression of slow and steady incremental refinement of the knowledge). His approach emphasized the non-linear and discontinuous nature of scientific evolution.
